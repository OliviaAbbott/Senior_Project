Data Analysis:

  1. fixed.acidity: the measurement of the total concentration of titratable acids and free hydrogen ions present in your wine. These acids, along with many more in smaller quantities, either occur naturally in the grapes or are created through the fermentation process.
ggplot() + geom_point(data = red_wine_data, aes(x=quality, y=fixed.acidity), colour = 'red', size = 1)

# Kernel density plots of fixed acidity
# grouped by quality ranking
qplot(fixed.acidity, data=red_wine_data, geom="density", fill=quality, alpha=I(.5),
   main="Distribution of quality on fixed.acidity level", xlab="fixed.acidity",
   ylab="Density")

# Boxplots of fixed acidity by quality ranking
# observations (points) are overlayed and jittered
qplot(quality, fixed.acidity, data=red_wine_data, geom=c("boxplot", "jitter"),
   fill=quality, main="fixed.acidity by Quality",
   xlab="", ylab="fixed.acidity")

qplot(quality, fixed.acidity, data=red_wine_data, geom="boxplot",
   fill=quality, main="fixed.acidity by Quality",
   xlab="", ylab="fixed.acidity")

  1. volatile.acidity: Volatile acidity is mostly caused by bacteria in the wine creating acetic acid and its byproduct, ethyl acetate. Volatile acidity could be an indicator of spoilage, or errors in the manufacturing processes. This is caused by things like damaged grapes, wine exposed to air, etc. This causes acetic acid bacteria to enter and thrive, and give rise to unpleasant tastes and smells.

  1. citric.acid: Citric acid is generally found in very small quantities in wine grapes. It acts as a preservative and is added to wines to increase acidity, complement a specific flavor or prevent ferric hazes. However, if too much is added to the wine, it can ruin the taste.

  1. chlorides: The amount of chlorides present in a wine is usually an indicator of its “saltiness.” This is usually influenced by the territory where the wine grapes grew, cultivation methods, and also the grape type. Too much saltiness is considered undesirable. The right proportion can make the wine more savory

  1. free.sulfur.dioxide: Sulfur dioxide exists in both free and bound forms. It’s the most common preservative used, usually added by wine makers to protect the wine from negative effects of exposure to air and oxygen. It acts as a sanitizing agent and adding it usually kills unwanted bacteria or yeast that might enter the wine and spoil its taste and aroma.

  1. density: Also known as specific gravity, it can be used to measure the alcohol concentration in wines. During fermentation, the sugar in the juice is converted into ethanol with carbon dioxide as a waste gas. Monitoring the density during the process allows for optimal control of this conversion step for highest quality wines. Sweeter wines generally have higher densities.
